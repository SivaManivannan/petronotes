## Two Phase p-T

Figure below shows the vapor-pressure curve for a typical hydrocarbon compound.

![[p-T single component system.png|498]]

- Above and to the left of the curve, the hydrocarbon behaves as a liquid; below and to the right it behaves as vapor.
- Saturated liquid and vapor coexist at every point along the vapor pressure curve.
- The curve ends at critical pressure and critical temperature of the hydrocarbon = Critical point

There is a sharp contrast in phase densities along the vapor pressure curve but as the temperature increases, this discontinuity becomes harder to discern.

Pure components exist as "saturated liquid + vapor" only along the vapor-pressure curve. At other pressures and temperatures, the component only behaves either like a liquid or a vapor.

## Two phase p-V

Instead p-T, the phase behavior can also be controlled via p-V (at constant T). Figure below shows the p-V diagram for ethane.
![[p-V single component system.png]]

- Point C represents the critical point.
- The saturation curve to the left of critical point (from Point A to Point C) defines the **bubble point** curve, along which component is a saturated liquid.
- Similarly, the saturation curve to the right of the critical point (Point B to Point C) defines the **dewpoint curve**, along which the component is a saturated vapor

> [!Tip] Bubble and dew point curves change with temperature

1. For any temperature less than the critical temperature, starting with a 100% vapor phase, successive decreases in volume at constant temperature elevates the vapor pressure until the saturation curve is reached and an infinitesimal volume of saturated liquid appears (point B).
2. Further decreases in volume (at constant temperature) proportionally increases the amount of saturated liquid without changing the pressure (pressure remains equal to the vapor pressure). While more liquid is being formed, the total volume (at Point D) is being reduced.
3. During this process the intensive properties like density of saturated liquid and saturated vapor remain constant as a consequence of the [[Gibbs Phase rule]] $(F=1)$.
4. Further decreases in volume will condense more liquid until bubble point is reached. Any further reduction in volume will result in 100% saturated liquid.
5. Further decreases in volume beyond the bubble point are accompanies by large increase in pressure (solid black line) because liquid is slightly compressible.
