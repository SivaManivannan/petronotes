## Classification of oil field systems

One might assume that the name used to identify a reservoir fluid should not affect how it is treated. Although this is true in theory, in practice petroleum engineers are required to identify reservoir fluids as either oil or gas for regulatory and other reasons.

Reservoir fluids are typically classified as

- Dry gas
- Wet gas
- Gas condensate
- Volatile oil
- Black oil

## Classification logic

This classification is determined by

1. the location of reservoir temperature with respect to the [[Critical Parameters|Critical Temperature]] and cricondentherm.
2. the location of first-stage separator temperature and pressure with respect to the phase diagram of the reservoir fluid.

> [!Important]
>
> - **Dry Gas** : Reservoir temperature is greater than cricondentherm and surface/transport conditions are outside the two phase envelope (A-A1)
> - **Wet gas** : Reservoir temperature is greater than cricondentherm but surface conditions are in the two phase envelope (A- A2)
> - **Gas condensate** : Reservoir temperature is less than cricondentherm but greater than critical temperature (B-B1-B2-B3)
> - **Volatile oil or Black oil** when the reservoir temperature is less than the mixture critical temperature (C-C1)

## Dry vs Wet vs Condensate Gas

The definition of a reservoir fluid as condensate gas or not depends purely on reservoir temperature and not surface process.

The definition of a reservoir fluid as dry or wet gas is strongly influenced by surface process. This makes the differentiation between dry and wet gas difficult because any gas can be cooled enough to condense a liquid phase.

Also, the classification of fluid as an oil (and not gas) is unambiguous because it only depends on the reservoir temperature being less than the critical temperature.

## Black oil vs Volatile oil

The distinction between a black oil and volatile oil is more arbitrary. Generally, volatile oils are characterized by high solution gas/oil ratio and exhibit large changes in fluid properties when the pressure is reduced only somewhere below the bubble point.
